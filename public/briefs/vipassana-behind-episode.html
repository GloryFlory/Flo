Ten Days of Silence —
What Vipassana Actually Taught Me

Not a spiritual pitch. Not a highlight reel. An honest account of the hardest thing I've ever done — and what stayed when I came back out.

Vipassana Meditation Silent Retreat Impermanence Integration Personal Growth

Before I went in, I asked my community what they wanted to know. People sent questions. Some were curious, some were sceptical, some were quietly thinking about doing one themselves. This episode is the answer.

A Vipassana retreat is ten days of noble silence — no talking, no phone, no eye contact, no reading or writing. You wake at 4am and meditate for roughly ten hours a day. Food and accommodation are free. The teacher is unpaid. It runs entirely on donation. The same structure, the same timetable, operates at centres all over the world.

I went into one in Vietnam with genuine fear. I wasn’t sure my body could handle it. I wasn’t sure my mind could either. I came out having completed the hardest thing I’ve ever done in my life.

Behind the Episode

Nobody tells you that the hardest part of a silent meditation retreat isn’t the silence. It’s not the 4am wake-ups, the ten hours of sitting, or the fact that you’ve handed your phone in at the door. It’s the moment you realise there’s nowhere left to go — no scroll, no conversation, no distraction. Just you, your mind, and everything you’ve been too busy to look at. I thought I’d lose it. Instead, I finished it. And it changed something.

The idea of doing a Vipassana had come up multiple times over the years — always through someone else. A friend mentioning they were about to go. Someone returning, quieter. And each time, my immediate reaction was fear. I thought I’d lose my mind. I had a specific physical worry too: I couldn’t sit still for more than an hour without back pain, and ten-day retreats mean ten hours of seated meditation daily.

What changed was a conversation in Hoi An with a sports therapist friend who had completed seven Vipassana retreats. He dismantled the myth that it required some kind of physical or mental pre-qualification. A few days later, a close friend told me they were about to do their first. Something crystallised. I sat with the idea for twenty minutes on a walk and signed up before I reached the coworking space.

Then, as if to confirm the decision: the apartment lease ended early, a flight from Da Nang got cancelled and the money refunded. Hanoi was where the retreat was held. The friction disappeared.

“

I walked past a sign that said Today is Day 1. I kept imagining what it would feel like when it said Day 9. I was genuinely terrified.

The first three days are brutal in a way that’s hard to communicate. Not because of dramatic psychological revelations — mostly the mind just opens its filing cabinet and starts throwing out random memories from twenty-five years ago. The real difficulty is physical: constant shifting, knee pain, back pain, minutes that feel like hours. People around me started leaving. By day three, two of the men near me in the hall had gone. It felt, strangely, like losing brothers.

Day four is the turning point. The first three days are spent learning Anapana — focusing attention on breath sensations around the nose, sharpening the mind’s ability to observe. On day four, you learn the Vipassana technique itself: scanning the entire body from head to toe, observing sensations without reacting to them.

The key discovery — something you can only understand by sitting with it — is that pain is not monolithic. Focus on the exact centre of knee pain, map it precisely across a few centimetres, and it fragments. Some areas more intense, some less, a specific epicentre. The moment you locate it with full attention, it diminishes. Not gone. Different. Your relationship to the sensation changes.

“

You’re not learning about impermanence. You’re sitting there proving it to yourself through your own knees and ankles for ten days straight.

This is what the evening discourses — recordings of S.N. Goenka, founder of the Vipassana Research Institute — describe as Anicca: impermanence. Every sensation arises. Every sensation passes. The practice is to observe this without craving the pleasant or resisting the painful. Equanimity. Not detachment. Not distance. Full presence without reactivity.

There was no magical moment where the pain disappeared. Right up to the final day, I was still shifting, still uncomfortable. But the difference between day three and day nine is not the presence of pain — it’s the relationship to it. On day three, I was counting minutes on the clock. On day nine, I was just sitting with it.

On day eleven, noble silence breaks. Reconnecting with the people who had sat beside me for ten days — people I had never spoken to, never made eye contact with — felt remarkably similar to coming down from psilocybin with someone: that same depth of recognition, the same sense of being truly seen by someone you’ve been through something intense with. No chemicals. Just ten days of shared radical attention.

Integration back into daily life is harder than the retreat itself. Some mornings the meditation lasts seven minutes instead of an hour. But the practice has something to say about that too: resist the gap between ambition and reality and you create more suffering. The fuse is longer. Reactions are milder. Things pass faster. That’s not failure to become enlightened. That’s the practice working.

Key Concepts
  • Vipassana A meditation technique meaning “to see things as they really are.” Rooted in the Theravada Buddhist tradition, popularised globally through the Vipassana Research Institute. Retreats are ten days, completely free, funded by donation from previous participants.
  • Anapana The preparatory practice taught in the first three days. Focuses sustained attention on breath sensations around the nose, sharpening the mind’s observational capacity before the main technique is introduced on day four.
  • Anicca Pali for impermanence. The understanding — experienced, not just known — that all phenomena arise and pass. Every sensation, every thought, every emotion. Central to why Vipassana works as a practice rather than a philosophy.
  • Noble Silence The code observed throughout the retreat: no talking, no eye contact, no non-verbal communication with other participants. Designed to turn attention fully inward and remove social performance from the experience.
  • Equanimity The quality the practice cultivates: observing sensation — pleasant or painful — without craving or aversion. Not detachment. Full presence, without the reaction.
